Wayfair's co-founder just sold $2.13 million in company shares — on the same day the stock was already 34% below its January 2026 peak. That single data point puts the entire narrative in sharp relief. Is this a founder who sees the ceiling? Or a routine portfolio move that the market is misreading? The SEC Form 4 filing dated February 26, 2026 holds the answer — if you know what to look for.

## Trend Breakdown

The trajectory of Wayfair's stock price over the past 14 months is a textbook boom-and-correction pattern. The stock spent most of 2023 and 2024 languishing below $50, bottoming at a 52-week low of $20.41, per Public.com data. Then 2025 happened. Wayfair's shares staged a 120% annual gain — per Motley Fool — propelled by improving financials, a narrowing net loss, and recovering e-commerce demand in home goods.

By January 2026, W had surged to a 52-week high of $119.98. That proved to be the peak. Since then, the reversal has been brutal: the stock closed at approximately $78.86 on February 26, 2026 — the same day as the insider sale — representing a 34.3% decline from that January high in under two months.

Zoom out to the year-to-date picture and the damage is 28% as of February 28, 2026, per Motley Fool. Three forces drove that drawdown: Q4 FY2025 earnings that underwhelmed market expectations despite a headline EPS beat, fresh tariff pressure on furniture importers following the Supreme Court's partial tariff ruling on February 20, and a broader macro environment — softening jobs data and rising oil prices — that punishes consumer discretionary stocks first and hardest.

The stock's current position relative to its own recent history is the critical context that makes the $2.13 million sale genuinely interesting.

## Comparison Breakdown

Two insider sales happened on the same date — February 26, 2026 — and the market treated them as a single signal. That deserves more precision.

Co-founder and co-chairman Steven Conine sold 26,950 shares at a weighted average price of $78.86, generating approximately $2.13 million in proceeds, per the SEC Form 4 filing. CEO Niraj Shah independently sold 27,300 shares on the same date at prices ranging from $77.23 to $80.32, also totaling roughly $2.13 million, per Investing.com. Both transactions were executed under Rule 10b5-1 trading plans — Conine's plan was adopted in May 2025, Shah's on May 29, 2025 — meaning both were pre-scheduled months in advance and are legally insulated from accusations of insider-knowledge trading.

Here is where the scale comparison becomes revealing. Conine's sale of 26,950 shares sits well below his recent-period median transaction size of 85,000 shares across 19 sales since May 2025, per Motley Fool. This sale was not a large-scale exit — it was a smaller-than-average liquidation event. Following the transaction, Conine retains 169,073 shares directly and 22,857 shares indirectly through SK Ventures LLC, per SEC Form 4 data. That is still a position worth approximately $15.2 million at the sale-date price of $78.86.

Comparatively, the Wall Street consensus price target for W currently sits at $104.44, per Public.com — implying 32% upside from the February 26 transaction price. Piper Sandler holds a $125 target with an Overweight rating, while Bernstein SocGen is more cautious at $100 with a Market Perform, per Investing.com. The divergence between insider selling activity and analyst price targets tells two very different stories about near-term conviction.

## What the Data Reveals

Strip away the headline and the financial data underneath Wayfair reveals a company in genuine transition — not freefall.

Revenue for full-year 2025 reached $12.5 billion, representing 5% year-over-year growth, per Motley Fool. Q4 FY2025 specifically showed a 6.9% year-over-year revenue increase, per Motley Fool. The net loss of $313 million in 2025 — while still red — represents a 36.4% improvement from the $492 million loss recorded in 2024. Q4 EPS came in at $0.85 against estimates of $0.40, a 112.5% beat, per Public.com. Free cash flow improved 40% year-over-year in the latest quarter.

Those numbers say the bottom-line trajectory is heading the right direction. What they do not explain is why the stock fell 28% in 2026 anyway. The answer sits in two places: margin concerns and macro headwinds. Stifel cut its price target from $100 to $89 following mixed Q4 results, specifically citing gross margin pressures, per Investing.com. Tariffs on furniture imports — couches, cabinets, vanities — remain in force after the Supreme Court's February 20 partial ruling, directly compressing Wayfair's cost structure on imported goods. Operating at a loss while facing rising input costs is not a comfortable position, regardless of the directional improvement.

The price-to-sales ratio near 1x is elevated relative to where it traded for most of 2025, per Motley Fool. That single valuation data point, combined with two consecutive quarters of net losses, creates a picture of a stock that ran ahead of its fundamentals last year and is now correcting toward a more realistic valuation floor.

## Outliers & Surprises

The most counterintuitive data point in this story is the Q4 EPS beat. Wayfair posted earnings per share of $0.85 — against analyst estimates of $0.40 — a 112.5% positive surprise, per Public.com. That is a massive beat by any standard. Yet the stock continued its 2026 decline. Markets were pricing something the headline EPS number did not capture: gross margin deterioration and first-quarter guidance that missed expectations, per Truist Securities.

The second anomaly is CEO Shah's concurrent $2.13 million sale. Two founders selling on the exact same date, under plans both adopted in May 2025, is statistically notable. It is entirely legal and mechanically explainable through 10b5-1 rules. But the optics of dual insider sales at a multi-month price low — when the stock had already shed 28% — is a data point serious investors track as a sentiment signal, even when the transactions were pre-scheduled.

One more outlier worth tracking: the next earnings release is expected on April 30, 2026, per Public.com, with an EPS estimate of just $0.04. If the gross margin pressure narrative proves worse than modeled, that number could fall below zero — and the stock will price it in before the filing date.

If current trends hold, Wayfair's financial story in 2026 is a race between improving operational metrics and compressing margins from tariff and macro headwinds. Full-year 2025 revenue of $12.5 billion growing at 5% year-over-year suggests a base-case trajectory toward $13–$13.1 billion in 2026 revenue, assuming no material demand shock.
